Stage 2 of 7
Understanding Sepsis — NICE NG51
Before acting, understand what you are dealing with. Sepsis has a precise clinical definition — and Joan fits it.
"Sepsis is a life-threatening organ dysfunction caused by a dysregulated host response to infection."
National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE), 2016, updated 2024. Guideline NG51: Sepsis: recognition, diagnosis and early management. [online] London: NICE.
⚠️
Why NG51 matters legally: NICE guidelines are not optional. NHS England (2017) mandates NG51 compliance. Failure to recognise and act on sepsis has resulted in coroner's inquests and NHS Serious Incident reports (Healthcare Safety Investigation Branch, 2019). This is not just best practice — it is your professional and legal duty.
🧬
Pathophysiology — click each step to expand
How infection became organ failure in Joan's case
1
Portal of entry — tap to expand
Cellulitis is a bacterial infection of the skin and subcutaneous tissues. In Joan's case, a break in the skin — common in diabetic patients with peripheral vascular disease — allowed Streptococcus pyogenes or Staphylococcus aureus to enter (Stevens et al., 2014). Poor peripheral circulation in diabetes delays local immune response, allowing the bacteria to proliferate.
🦵 Joan: diabetic foot changes + PVD = impaired local immunity. Bacteria multiply unchecked.
2
Bacteraemia
As local control fails, bacteria enter the bloodstream — this is bacteraemia. The initial immune response is appropriate but becomes dysregulated. Pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-1, IL-6) are released in excessive quantities, triggering a systemic inflammatory response (Singer et al., 2016).
🩸 Joan: overnight worsening despite IV antibiotics suggests bacteraemia established before treatment.
3
Vasodilation + Hypoperfusion
Cytokines cause systemic vasodilation and increased vascular permeability. Plasma leaks into tissues, intravascular volume falls, and blood pressure drops. Cardiac output rises (tachycardia) to compensate but is insufficient — tissues are under-perfused and hypoxic (Hotchkiss et al., 2016).
💓 Joan: BP 88/52, HR 118 — classic compensated shock. SpO₂ 93% = inadequate O₂ delivery to tissues.
4
Organ Dysfunction
Sustained hypoperfusion causes cellular hypoxia and organ dysfunction. The brain (confusion), kidneys (oliguria), and cardiovascular system (hypotension) are most vulnerable early. Lactate rises as cells switch to anaerobic metabolism — lactate ≥ 2 mmol/L signals organ hypoperfusion per NICE NG51 (NICE, 2016).
🧠 Joan: confused (brain), 120mL urine in 8h (kidneys), BP 88/52 (cardiovascular). Multiple organ dysfunction.
5
Septic Shock (if untreated)
Septic shock is defined by NICE NG51 as sepsis with persistent hypotension despite adequate fluid resuscitation, and lactate > 2 mmol/L. Mortality rises from ~10% in early sepsis to 40–60% in septic shock (Seymour et al., 2016). Every hour of delay in treatment increases mortality by approximately 7% (Kumar et al., 2006).
⚠️ Joan is on the threshold. Her BP has not responded to oral fluids. Without immediate action, this will become septic shock.

Stage 5 of 7
SBAR: Calling for Help
You need to call Dr Patel (registrar on call) immediately. Use the SBAR framework to communicate clearly and confidently. Read the model answer, then write your own version.
🎯
Communication is a patient safety issue. NHS England (2012) Patient Safety Alert PS001 identifies poor clinical communication as the leading cause of avoidable deterioration. SBAR (Situation–Background–Assessment–Recommendation) is the NHS-mandated structure for clinical escalation (Leonard et al., 2004).
📞
Model SBAR call — Joan Evans
Study this example, then practise your own version below
S
Situation
"Dr Patel, I'm calling about Mrs Joan Evans, 68 years old, bed 4, Ward 7. I am very concerned — I believe she is developing sepsis. She is confused, hypotensive with a BP of 88/52, and her NEWS2 is 12. I need you to come and review her urgently."
B
Background
"She was admitted yesterday afternoon with left leg cellulitis and started on IV Flucloxacillin. She has Type 2 Diabetes, hypertension, and peripheral vascular disease. She has a documented penicillin allergy. Her condition has worsened overnight."
A
Assessment
"Heart rate is 118, respiratory rate 22, SpO₂ 93% on air, temperature 38.9°C, AVPU is V — she is responding only to voice. Urine output has been only 120mL in the last 8 hours. Her lactate has not yet been checked. I am concerned she may be in early septic shock."
R
Recommendation
"I need you to review her immediately — within the next 15 minutes. I am applying high-flow oxygen now and preparing an IV fluid bolus. We will need urgent bloods, blood cultures, a VBG for lactate, and a new antibiotic prescription given her allergy. Can you attend now?"

Gibbs' Reflective Cycle (1988) — applied to this simulation

Description — What happened?
Joan Evans was admitted with cellulitis and deteriorated overnight into sepsis with a NEWS2 of 12, multiple red flags, and early organ dysfunction. As the morning nurse, you identified the deterioration, calculated NEWS2, applied the Sepsis Six, and escalated using SBAR. Cellulitis — a relatively common diagnosis — had become a life-threatening emergency.
Feelings — What were you thinking and feeling?
Common responses in simulations like this include: anxiety at the severity, uncertainty about whether your concern is "justified", pressure not to bother the doctor, and urgency once the picture becomes clear. It is normal to feel uncertain. The key professional skill is acting despite uncertainty when patient safety is at risk.
Evaluation — What was good? What was difficult?
Good: recognition of NEWS2 elevation; application of Sepsis Six; identification of the penicillin allergy; structured SBAR communication. Difficult: the uncertainty of acting fast without full information; overcoming hesitancy to escalate; communicating urgency assertively without a full diagnosis confirmed.
Analysis — Why did things happen as they did?
Sepsis is frequently missed because the initial diagnosis (cellulitis) creates cognitive anchoring — a well-documented patient safety issue (Croskerry, 2002). Once a "label" exists, deviation from it requires explicit re-assessment. Systematic tools (NEWS2, Sepsis Six, SBAR) exist precisely to overcome cognitive bias and ensure consistent, guideline-driven care (Seymour et al., 2016).
Conclusion — What else could you have done?
Earlier awareness that diabetic patients with cellulitis are at higher sepsis risk may have prompted more frequent monitoring. A proactive "if-this-then-that" plan at handover ("if NEWS2 rises above 5, call me") is good practice. Reviewing local sepsis protocols before your shift ensures you can act without delay.
Action plan — What will you do next time?
(1) On every shift: identify your highest-risk patients (infection + comorbidity). (2) Know your ward's sepsis pathway before you need it. (3) Practice SBAR regularly — confident communication is a skill. (4) Reference NICE NG51 and the UK Sepsis Trust 'Sepsis Six' tool. (5) Consider completing the UK Sepsis Trust e-learning module.

Analysis: Cognitive anchoring in clinical practice
Why good nurses miss sepsis — and how to prevent it

Joan arrived with a clear diagnosis: cellulitis. This is a well-documented trigger for cognitive anchoring bias — once a diagnosis is established, clinical deterioration is often attributed to it rather than prompting reconsideration (Croskerry, 2002). Research by Shulman et al. (2018) found that 44% of patients who developed sepsis on medical wards had been admitted with a skin infection as the primary diagnosis.

The countermeasure is systematic clinical assessment — NEWS2 is not optional. It forces a numbers-based re-evaluation that bypasses cognitive anchoring. The NMC Code (2018) requires nurses to "recognise and respond compassionately to the needs of those who are deteriorating." Systematic deterioration tracking is therefore not just good practice — it is a professional duty.

Joan's outcome
The Sepsis Six was delivered within 42 minutes of recognition. Joan received IV Meropenem (penicillin-sparing), fluid resuscitation, and high-flow oxygen. Her lactate was 3.8 mmol/L. She was transferred to HDU and discharged to the ward after 4 days. She was discharged home on day 9. Session debrief — Key learning points
🧠
Diagnosis ≠ reassurance
Cellulitis is the source of sepsis — not an alternative to it. Every patient with infection must be re-assessed for systemic deterioration at every contact.
⏰
Time is the critical variable
Mortality increases ~7% per hour of antibiotic delay (Kumar et al., 2006). The Sepsis Six is a bundle — partial delivery is insufficient. All 6 elements within 1 hour.
💊
Allergies can kill — check first
Joan's penicillin allergy was in her notes but the night nurse gave Flucloxacillin. Always verify allergies before prescribing. This is a never event. The antibiotic choice changed everything.
📞
Assertive escalation saves lives
UK Sepsis Trust (2019) data shows that nurse-initiated escalation using SBAR reduces time-to-treatment by an average of 23 minutes compared to non-structured communication.
🔢
NEWS2 is a safety net
Without NEWS2, a nurse might have recorded "slightly unwell" in free text. The score of 12 is unambiguous and mandates an unambiguous response. Systemic tools remove hesitancy.
